Introduction

Field Day is a one day unconference for leaders of technical student communities. It brings together leaders from different regional communities to hang out, become friends, learn from each other's mistakes and successes, and solve shared problems.

Overview Video

open in new window

Motivation

Separate communities frequently co-exist in a shared region. They know of each other, but they don't collaborate or talk to each other much. If they do, they would benefit from doing it even more.

Their relationship tends to be underdeveloped for the following reasons:

  • Members of each communities' committee/organizing team don't know each other
  • They don't frequently communicate. Unlike their peers in their own community, they don't see each other in classes every day.
  • Kicking off, and then sustaining inter-community collaboration efforts is a hard task.
  • There is not enough opportunity to maintain inter-community friendships over time.

Field Day can help improve some of these problems. At the very fundamental level it represents a place for student leaders across communities to spend time together, which will improve their relationships.

What's an unconference?

To quote the google definition:

A loosely structured conference emphasizing the informal exchange of information and ideas between participants

There are two key points to what an unconference is in the context of Field Day:

  1. It's (largely) unstructured - there is no CFP. Topics of discussion can be decided on the day.
  2. The content itself is decided and provided by the attendees on the day.

There are two reasons why the unconference format works well for Field Day:

  1. Since attendees decide the content, it is timely, relevant, and represents what they want to talk about.
  2. This rather loose structure carries a much smaller organizational burden than a full conference.

Feedback from attendees after Beta Field Day was that they really appreciated this structure, level of autonomy, and self-direction.

What's a student leader?

A student leader is someone who's actively involved in improving their local technical community. They might be the president or chairperson of their school's tech club or be one of the organizers of the annual hackathon. They might run the student hardware group or be actively involved in introducing other students to programming.

All student leaders are potential Campus Experts.

Why is GitHub doing this?

GitHub is on a mission to empower student developers. Technical student communities are a proven and effective way for student developers to find empowerment. Field Day helps leaders of those communities to be even more effective. It's also an amazing opportunity to tell them about Campus Experts.

What does regional mean? How big is a region?

We're still figuring out what exactly that means, but here is a rough guideline:

  • No-one should really be traveling for more than 4 hours to get to Field Day.
  • There should be a sufficient density of student leaders for at least 40 Field Day attendees, given the above constraint.

Size - How many people should attend Field Day?

40 - 100 people (probably not more than 100)

Financials

GitHub will provide 100 USD per expected attendee. So if you're planning for 40 people to attend, you will have access to $4000. If you're planning for 75 attendees, you will have access to $7500

Please also keep track of your actual expenditure.

What should this money be used for?

  • Venue (unless you can use a university venue for free)
  • Food & Drinks
  • Photographer (pictures are superb for preserving the memories everyone will make at Field Day and they're awesome marketing material as well)
  • Decorations
    • We had both balloons and cheap umbrellas in multiple colors at Beta Field Day and it was magical ✨
  • Utensils you may need on the day (scissors, door stops, etc.)

How will I receive this money?

After you've created a budget that approximates how much you spend and what you spend on, you can invoice GitHub for the amount you need. Follow the normal Campus Experts expensing process and in your email to invoices@github.com include your invoice.

Organizing team

The organizing team consists of multiple Campus Experts. If a Field Expert is available in the region, they will lead the organizing team. The organizing team should include at least another 2 members from 2 other regional communities. For larger regions, we've seen 6-8 Experts being part of the Organizing Team

We strongly recommend this for the following reasons:

  • Shared workload
  • Making sure Field Day is appealing and inclusive of all regional communities, not just one.
  • Student leaders from a community are more likely to attend if one of their team was involved in the organization of Field Day.

Format - On Day structure

The picture above shows our schedule for Beta Field Day. You're of course free to adjust this based on what works best in the region. Basic building blocks that should be included:

  • There should be a "Welcome from GitHub" slot at the beginning (This will be delivered by you as the GitHub Field Expert)
  • There should be an icebreaker session at the beginning
  • The majority of Field Day should be in the unconference format (breakout discussions & lightning talks)
  • There should be a feedback session at the end, that also gives folks a chance to reflect on what they've learned

Volunteers

You will likely need some volunteers to help you out on the day. The recommended number of volunteers is 1 per 10 expected attendees, so if you're planning for 50 attendees, there should be 5 volunteers (this includes the organizers).

What do volunteers do?

There are multiple duties volunteers may undertake throughout the day and assigning those is at your discretion and may differ based on venue, number of available attendees, etc. Volunteer duties:

  • Take notes and watch for code of conduct violations in breakout discussion sessions (make sure to assign one volunteer per breakout session)
  • Set up Food & Drinks
  • Buy food in case of special dietary requirements not met by the food provided
  • Participate in the discussions (volunteers are not excluded from participation)
  • Run the ice breaker session
  • Handling registration

You should brief volunteers in the morning of the event and remind them of their duties. Feel free to borrow from the Volunteer Briefingopen in new window we used at Beta Field Day.

Hallway track

Field Day is a place for student leaders to meet, hang out, and collaborate. The best way for them to do that might be outside of any particular schedule item. The simple fact that they're in the same room may be enough for them to interact meaningfully. Because of this, it is absolutely vital to inform attendees that they can embark on the "hallway track" (where they hang out in the hallway as opposed to attending a session).
This is the reason why we had a 2h lunch break at Beta Field Day and a space for student leaders to interact outside of any scheduled sessions.

"Unconference" you say, why is there a keynote in the Beta Field Day Schedule?

We recommend kicking off the event with a keynote, where an experienced community builder shares their learnings and offers a different perspective on the world of community management than perhaps most student leaders are opposed to in their tenure.

The intention for the keynote is to encourage and inspire further and broader discussions later in the day.

At Beta Field Day we heard from Justin Johnsonopen in new window (over 10 years experience in community management) about how "Building Communities is Investing in the Future" Watch it on YouTubeopen in new window

Venue - What is required?

  • One large space where everyone can sit (for welcome session, keynote, etc.)
    • This should have AV equipment (microphone, speakers, projector)
  • Breakout spaces (to facilitate discussions)
    • 1 breakout space per 25 expected attendees
    • large space for everyone can be a breakout space too
    • Each space should have a whiteboard and ideally a projector/TV
  • Space for "Learn about Campus Experts" lounge (this consists of a few couches, a coffee table, and some standup posters)
  • WiFi
  • Whiteboard for discussion matrix (picture below)

"Learn about Campus Experts" lounge

One part of the venue should contain the "Learn about Campus Experts" lounge, ideally in an open space.
It should consist of a few couches, a table (with octocat stickers), and the Campus Expert banners we'll send you.
The idea is that throughout the event, attendees can come to this lounge and relax as well as learn more about how becoming a Campus Expert can help them improve their community.
If possible, a Campus Expert attending should be present to answer questions about the programme.
